Gratitude's Resonance - Reflecting on IMSF



When I was back in Minnesota for winter break
I made 2024 vision boards with my closest friends
Not because we believed in “manifesting”
Just as a way to spend time together
To subtly celebrate everything we were working on
in school and in life.
We each signed one another’s boards.
These four collages were then packed into suitcases and spread across
four countries USA Canada Ireland Thailand
Looking at mine now
I can see how much of it focuses on IMSF and the earnestness behind each goal despite its silly tone
Excerpts from a 2024 vision board:
Supreme Leader
Admittedly I didn’t apply to the program seeking social change or leadership skills
I was only looking to continue the work I started in Professor Faraj and Anand’s class
and connect my university learning to real world experience
Though these intentions may seem
misplaced they were sincere
The idea of being a leader made me uncomfortable even if I didn’t want it to
Eight months later
while I still hesitate to call myself a leader or a rockstar
I can tell you what leadership means to me and feel the potential to be one in the future
One of the first lessons I learned about leadership is the importance of listening
Before the course, I had regarded listening as a valuable skill
but I had underestimated its power in leading change
Leadership can be glamorized as the notion that brilliant minds are struck
with brilliant ideas
and it is those “following” who do the listening We experienced first hand in our community consultations just how incorrect that portrayal can be
To truly understand the problem we needed to listen to people’s first hand experiences
This is also where we encountered our first problem
In trying so hard to ask the right questions and make the most of our interviews, we failed to listen to their answers
I remember one conversation we had with Ajung Moon
she suggested that analyzing access to technology and compute would be more influential in actually addressing inequality
At that point we were already so far into the project that we didn’t hear her
I can’t definitely say if this is the direction our project should have gone in
but I can recognize there were moments in which we were too narrowly focused
We also could have done a better job listening by engaging with a larger variety of people
Expert opinions were valuable, but they often framed the problem in one specific way
By limiting our interview pool to people with certain experiences, I feel we neglected those we were trying to help much like we accused big tech companies of doing
I think we understood the listening exercises and lecture on framing the problem at the time yet we struggled to effectively apply those two concepts in our project
The other thing that stands out to me about leadership is the ability to create momentum
For much of my high school and early college
I was under the assumption that if I followed the right steps
and waited for the right opportunity then things would fall into place
Now I firmly believe in my ability to make things happen
This realization hit me when we interviewed Anouk Ruhaak
I had admired her work for some time and secretly hoped to follow in her footsteps of sorts
but before IMSF, I would have continued to stalk her website
maybe read about her work in my free time In my mind I was waiting until I had a certain skill set or position or experience to warrant reaching out to her
Thanks to IMSF, I simply sent her an email no more than a week and a half later we were interviewing her for our project
Realizing I had the power to go after the things I wanted more actively and try to make them happen was an amazing feeling
At the winter retreat, David Lenke spoke about the importance of maintaining the “why”
but allowing the “how” to change
It is rallying around this evermoving “how” that requires momentum and ultimately a leader
As our initiative continued it took more effort to create momentum within the five of us in order to keep figuring out the “how”
Another quote from David Lenke’s speech that has stuck with me is
“If something is worth doing, it’s worth doing badly.”
I wish someone had said this to me three years ago! (Although maybe I wouldn’t have been ready to hear it.)
So much of my waiting for things to happen, is waiting for something to be
perfect But waiting for the perfect “how” means you might never start
This is absolutely true with our impact initiative.
I would have waited until after school to even begin thinking about things like data transparency let alone trying to create a solution
What a waste!
I’ve learned
SO
MUCH
about the problem
and about myself
in the process of doing it badly (well not actually badly, just certainly not perfectly)

A Dream Team
Putting “A Dream Team” on my vision board was easy because I already had it!
I was lucky enough to work with four beyond wonderful people
each with their own skills
and equally committed to the project and the process
the value of our team isn’t necessarily in the diversity of our skills or perspective, but in our dedication to keep showing up and determine what comes next.
Their commitment has made our team incredibly special to me.
This was my first experience of truly collaborative group work the project felt like ours rather than mine alone
Though, in doing everything together, we could have been more efficient and organized with our time
And sometime we were overly focused on problem-solving
this created an artificial urgency that prevented us from reflecting And ultimately from listening
There was a shift in the group dynamic during the second semester
As we began to shape our project, I do feel we sometimes had conflicting priorities
When the impact initiative went from a project to a startup itself, I had trouble vocalizing my concerns
I also felt there was an underlying assumption that as a computer science student I would be more responsible for the content of the guide Even though I was just as uncertain as everyone else
I felt uncomfortable using a guide that didn’t exist as the foundation for a startup
As a result
my participation in the second semester was performative at times; I didn’t have the same energy or enthusiasm
My reservations about the project definitely impacted morale in the group as I have a terrible poker face And there were moments it did get in the way of us moving forward
Despite this, I am still unbelievably proud of what my team accomplished and presented for showcase day
but I also know there is room for improvement
there is more work for us to do and I can't wait to start

Research Legend
I included this because, like leadership, I still felt quite uncomfortable after that first semester
Despite my best efforts to be proactive to get the research project started, we certainly had a few setbacks
I had covid then the PhD student I was supposed to work with, Gina, unfortunately had an accident and was unavailable for the foreseeable future (she is okay now!)
By the time I was able to meet with Professor Faraj and Anand to course correct, it was clear the project would be quite different from what I had originally expected
With Gina, I would have been learning qualitative research skills and assisting the coding process of her ethnographic study Now, I would be exploring some data posted by the Quebec Ministry of Health and Social Services
I became overly worried about trying to figure out what I was supposed to do, what the right answer was (sounds familiar?)
I never considered what I could uniquely contribute or what I wanted to contribute
My nervousness and unease came through.
I still produced an analysis of the data
I learned a great deal about the Quebec health system
I improved my R programming skills but I can’t confidently say I managed-up
If I had the opportunity The research portion of the program is what I most wish I could redo
My dad also had an unexpected health emergency at the beginning of second semester
I was able to keep up with coursework and the impact initiative (for the most part)
but I neglected the research project for about a month
I was able to ask Anand for help and finish the project with his support but I'm not exceptionally proud of my contribution to their research
I definitely learned more about the process and how it compares to working in industry
but I also know I want to try another semester to be more engaged with the work before deciding if I might like to pursue it on my own I think I could do a better job, and contribute more creatively at each step, now knowing how open-ended the process is
I certainly have positive takeaways from the project too
I am incredibly grateful for Anand’s assistance throughout the year
Not only did he provide direction for the research project itself, but he routinely checked in and gave advice about my other courses and interests I don’t know what I would have done without him or the time he was willing to give me each week, especially second semester
I enjoyed participating in Bricolab meetings with the whole team
Being able to see other people at various stages of the research process and the types of things they were working on was probably the most helpful to seeing what research is like
One meeting has stood out to me
This particular discussion was led by a special guest from another university about how scholarship, research, and academia will be impacted by AI
I’m not a scholar, researcher, or academic; I still strongly identify with being a student but I feel lucky I got to be in the room for such an interesting conversation, with my own questions and comments to add too
I remember a particular comment about how we can treat reading as a technology and consider its transformative impact on the skills we practice and privilege now
in my head I just kept thinking “Yes!!!
This is the kind of stuff I want to talk about
learn about
work on
This is the room I want to be in!”
So if that meeting is any indication of what research may be like I definitely need to try again
While I felt relatively unsatisfied about my participation in the project as a whole I still liked the work I was able to contribute
I really enjoyed being able to present on showcase day
One of the values in learning for me is how I can share that process with other people
presenting reminded me there are lots of ways I can engage in that outside of class so much so that I'll be teaching piano lessons back in Minnesota this summer


Immune to Time
By winter break I had come to realize how wonderful an opportunity the IMSF was
Viewing the semester solely as a set of deadlines makes it flash by without any time for me to reflect
I didn’t want to continue repeating this pattern
(though surprise, I did, but at least to a lesser degree than usual)
So when I set a goal to be “Immune to time”, I was essentially setting a goal to make the most of this experience to enjoy it as much as possible
Showcase day as a whole was a moment to reflect
I was just immensely grateful for the chance to celebrate the hard work we all put in
Looking around the room, I could see the dedication and effort that both students and faculty had invested, and it was incredibly rewarding to have a day just to say thank you and congratulations
College can feel like a marathon, and sometimes we forget to celebrate the small wins along the way Thank you for making the day feel so special
Moreover, I was just filled with awe and pride at the amazing IMSF fellows!
We spent so much time stressing over our own project that I completely forgot there were 60 other people doing the same thing
Actually seeing the presentations, the ways in which each team was creative and addressed the problem differently even if they shared an SDG was so motivating I just can’t wait to see what they’ll do next
I’m far from immune — all I want is more time
More time to work on the impact initiative
more time to learn from my IMSF peers simply more of everything!
A year ago, I was seriously considering dropping out of school I was struggling to see the value in what I was learning or why
Fast forward to today, and everyday for the past some odd months
I wake up filled with gratitude for the opportunity to simply try.
to listen
to lead
to do something badly to do the things worth doing
Maybe this sounds like an underwhelming revelation
but it couldn’t be farther from my mindset one year ago
Then, I was combative
framing everything as a test in which I needed to prove myself
And while that combative nature is still very much within me I also believe every single moment I am here learning and growing is a gift.
Now please bear with me, but I can tangibly feel the shift in my mindset
I’ve been meditating for almost two years now
after the worries of the week and stresses of the day are let go
underneath lies a well of gratitude
of resilience
of courage that wasn't there before
Some days it’s more present than others but regardless, I can’t overstate how important that growth is to me
This change was unconsciously reflected in my new values
Intuition and Wonder have remained important to me
But now so are gratitude and courage which I hadn’t even considered in the top 10 the first time we did the exercise
If I want to be a leader in the future
It will take courage within myself to keep trying
but also a deep sense of gratitude
for the people and community who make it possible And ultimately make my courage mean something.
